  • 1912 : Literature, Stockholm

    Pierre de Coubertin (France), Ode to Sport, Gold, Germany

    O Sport, you are Peace! ...

    Baron Pierre de Coubertin founded the modern Olympic Games in 1896 and began organizing the Olympic Art Competitions ten years later. The IOC resolved to create five contests--a Pentathlon of the Muses--in architecture, literature, music, painting, and sculpture. Every four years, competitors would submit artworks based on sport or inspired by Olympic ideas.

    Coubertin submitted Ode to Sport to the first contest in Stockholm in 1912 under two pseudonyms and two languages: French and German. The jury awarded him the gold over better-known writers like Gabriele d'Annunzio, but it had reservations about the bilingual model: It's preferable for presented works to carry the mark of a national genius and not to try to cross borders.

    As such, the gold was given to Germany, rather than both nations. And although future artists would sometimes win Olympic medals in pairs or groups, the groups were never international, in spite of Coubertin's hopeful example.

  • 1920 : Literature, Antwerp

    Theodore Andrea Cook, Olympic Games of Antwerp, Silver, Great Britain

    ' We are the dead! ' he cried, / whom you commemorate.

    The 1916 Olympics were cancelled due to World War I. During that time, Coubertin had to defend the decision to keep Germany in the IOC; Britain's Theodore Andrea Cook resigned in protest. In the end, the Olympics did not invite Germany to participate in the 1920 or 1924 Games.

    Cook's silver-medal poem, Olympic Games of Antwerp, dedicates the Games to the soldiers who died near the host city. Olympic artists would evoke the heroism of the Great War, but Cook was one of the few to depict its carnage. His poem ends with a vision of the trenches.

  • 1924 : Painting, Paris

    TJack B. Yeats, Swimming (The Liffey Swim), Silver, Ireland

    IIn 1924, the Olympics and the art competitions came to Paris--the capital of the European avant-garde and modern art. The rulebook announced that around 150 judges from Paris's cosmopolitan artistic community would take part, including names like Ravel, Stravinsky, Sargent, Maeterlinck, Valéry, and Wharton.

    Jack B. Yeats, brother of W.B. Yeats, earned a silver for Swimming.

    The painting exhibits Yeats's recent experiments in expressionism, a modernist movement characterized by its attempt to convey psychological experience and (in painting) by its rough brushstrokes and moody colors. Like many other Olympic artists from 1924, Yeats takes up the modernist fascination with crowds and captures the feeling of a Dublin cheering section.

  • 1928 : Painting, Amsterdam

    Angel Zàrraga, Blonde Soccer Player and Rugby Scene, No Prize, Mexico

    Angel Zàrraga was the only Mexican artist to compete in the 1928 Amsterdam Olympics--likely because he was working in Paris at the time. He submitted 16 paintings to the contest, which would have been much harder to do from overseas. In spite of the volume of submissions, not one of Zàrraga's works made the podium. As was typical of other years, the winners were all Europeans or Americans.

    Nevertheless, Zàrraga's works highlight the changing demographics of the Olympics, by depicting female athletes as well as Latin American athletes. In spite of Coubertin's resistance to including women's sports in the Games, ten new women's events were added in 1928. Meanwhile, the Olympics had expanded their Games globally, by promoting minor tournaments on all continents.

    Starting in 1928, there were more medals to be won. The five arts were divided into sub-categories: Painting, for example, had new medals for drawings and water colors or graphic arts.

  • 1932 : Architecture, Los Angeles

    Walter Gropius, Project for Sports Complex in Halle/Saale, No prize, Germany

    The Los Angeles art competitions were the Walter Gropius's name is buried in the long list of losers. In spite of his worldwide fame as the founder of the Bauhaus school, his plan for a modern civic athletics complex lost to much more old-fashioned architectural projects.

    Throughout the Olympic Art Competitions, this was not uncommon: modern, famous competitors frequently lost to unknown artists working in more traditional styles. In other years, popular composer and educator Emile Jacques-Dalcroze, bestselling writer Henry de Montherlant, war-poet Robert Graves, and Dada painter Georges Grosz all entered the art competitions and fell short of the podium.

  • 1936 : Music, Berlin

    Bunya Koh (Taiwan), Formosan Dance, Honorable Mention, Japan/center>

    In the 1930s, Japan emerged as a new major competitor, submitting to every art category except literature with a total of nearly 150 artworks. Up against western artworks and evaluated by western juries, these Japanese artists struggled to get recognition proportional to their participation, but they did get two medals and two Honorable Mentions in 1936.

    Bunya Koh was one of the honored artists. Like his other work, Formosan Dance combines different east Asian musical traditions as well as elements of European modernism.

    Music turned out to be the least successful category of the Olympic Art Competitions: this branch never attracted large numbers or top talent. Still, the Olympics did inspire unofficial or non-competing musical works by Darius Milhaud, Arthur Honegger, and Richard Strauss.

    1936 : Sculpture, Berlin

    Farpi Vignoli(Italy), Sulky Driver, Gold, Italy

    The most successful nations throughout the art competitions were Germany, Italy, and France. Many national teams benefited strongly from hosting, and Germany benefited the most: during the Berlin Games, the local team won 12 of its 24 art medals.

    These numbers are undeniably suspicious. The Berlin Games of 1936 were designed as international propaganda for the Nazis and their allies, and the art competitions were no exception. The inauguration program to the exhibition contained a prefatory address by Propaganda Minister Joseph Goebbels. We also see Goebbels in the photograph of Farpi Vignoli's Sulky Driver

    More experimental artists, deemed degenerate by the Nazis, staged their own protest exhibition in Amsterdam in 1936, called The Olympics Under Dictatorship.

  • 1948 : London

    Victoria and Albert Museum Exhibition, England

    The Olympics were cancelled again in 1940 and 1944, during World War II. After the war, Japan and Germany were not invited to participate in the London Games. Instead, a number of new nations arrived, making these the most global art contests to date. The map pictured shows all the submissions for 1948.

    London was the last city to host the art competitions. During preparations for 1952, IOC President Avery Brundage opposed continuing the contests because they rewarded professionals, when Olympic events were supposed to be restricted to amateurs.

    Since then, the Cultural Olympiad--a celebration of local traditions run by the host-city--has taken up the former role of the art competitions. Coubertin once claimed that the one difference between our Olympiads and plain sporting championships was the contests of art--but today, even without the competitions, the arts continue to distinguish the Olympic Games from other sporting events.
